Cappella Palatina

The palatine chapel is located on the first floor of the Palazzo Reale in Palermo. The small basilica was commissioned by Roger II of Sicily and was built on the ruins of an earlier church from 1080 and finished in 1143. It is dedicated to St Peter and the architecture is in the usual Byzantine architecture with 6 pointed arches resting on classical columns, possible from the previous church.

According to some accounts, the church was originally a building on its own. In time the Palazzo Reale developed around it to eventually include the church in its structure.

The chapel combines the Norman Architecture with the Arabic arches and script of the roof using the 8 pointed stars stalactite arches, and the byzantine dome and golden mosaics. One important feature is the muqarnas roof made of wood where each facet is painted by hand with everyday scenes and zoomorphic designs.

Fontana Pretoria

This fountain originates from Florence and was designed by Francesco Camilliani in 1554 and transported to Palermo in 1574. The fountain has 48 statues depicting both men and women as well as heads of animals and fictional characters. Originally created for the garden of Don Luigi de Toledo in Florence, the indebted owner sold the fountain to the city of Palermo before moving to Naples.

When the fountain arrived in Palermo, some of the statues were missing – it is believed that Don Luigi kept some which are now on display in Florence Museum and a lot of the other statues were damaged in transport.

The son of the original sculptor Camillo Camilliani with the help of Michelangelo Naccherino helped with re-assembling the fountain in Palermo and completing the damaged and missing works.

Bibliography: wikipedia.com
Visiting hours:
The fountain is a free to see monument in Palermo.

Cattedrale di Palermo

The earliest church on this site was founded by Pope Gregory I and later turned into a mosque in the 9th century conquest. The new cathedral was erected in 1185 by Walter Ophamilr, the norman archibishop of Palermo. The church was expanded over the years with the corner towers being built in the 14th and 15th century while the current neoclassical appearance with the adding of the domes was done between 1781 and 1801.

The cathedral was also an early type of a solar observatory used to correctly measure the days and the sun movement through the seasons.

In Palermo, the day used to start and end at sunrise. However the times were not the same as in the St Peter Basilica in Rome and therefore the church could not offer the exact time when the Spring Equinox occurred to provide the correct date for Easter. This solar observatory was built in 1801 and constituted of a hole in the roof measuring the sun path on the floor along certain measures showing the day and time.

Bibliography: wikipedia.com, monrealeduomo.it

Church of Santa Caterina

The church of Saint Catherine of Alexandria was built in 1310 as a result of the last will of the rich Benvenuta Mastrangelo. The female monastery under the direction of the Dominican order was erected in the place where the palace of George of Antioch, admiral of Roger II, once stood. The church we see today was rebuilt in 1532 when a wider building was needed. The current style is attributed to architects Francesco Ferrigo, Francesco Camilliani and Antonio Muttone.

The church houses many precious oil paintings and frescoes as well as beautiful inlaid marble floors and balustrades.

Bibliography: Wikipedia.com
